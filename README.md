The Mechanics: How It Works
To properly evaluate the JetHose, it is essential to understand its mechanical design and how it is intended to function. At its core, the JetHose is a hose nozzle attachment — a device that fits onto the end of a standard garden hose and modifies the way water exits the nozzle. The fundamental principle behind any nozzle is straightforward: by narrowing the aperture through which water flows, you increase the velocity of the water stream. This is basic fluid dynamics, governed by the principle of continuity, which states that as the cross-sectional area of a flow decreases, the velocity must increase to maintain the same volumetric flow rate.
The JetHose was designed to attach to standard 3/4-inch and 1/2-inch garden hoses, which are the most common fittings found on household outdoor taps across the UK and many other markets. The attachment mechanism typically involves a screw-on connector that tightens onto the hose end, creating a seal that prevents leakage during use. The Physics Gap: Reality Check
This is perhaps the most critical section of this investigation, because it addresses the fundamental claim at the heart of the JetHose marketing: that it can deliver professional-grade pressure washing performance. To evaluate this claim properly, we need to examine the physics of water pressure and understand the limitations inherent in any passive nozzle attachment connected to a standard household water supply.
Standard garden hoses in the UK and most residential settings operate at water pressures ranging from approximately 40 to 60 PSI (pounds per square inch). This is the pressure delivered by the municipal water supply or a typical domestic pump system to your outdoor tap. It is the maximum pressure available to any device connected to that tap, unless that device includes its own pump or pressure-boosting mechanism. A standard garden hose nozzle, including the JetHose, is a passive device — it has no motor, no pump, and no external power source.
PSI from Garden Hose
Standard household water pressure available at a typical outdoor tap in the UK.
PSI from Pressure Washer
Operating pressure range of professional electric and gas-powered pressure washers.
PSI Added by Nozzle
A passive nozzle cannot multiply input pressure — it can only redirect existing flow.
Industrial and consumer pressure washers, by contrast, generate pressures ranging from 1,500 to 3,000 PSI or more. They achieve this through an integrated pump — either electric or petrol-driven — that actively pressurizes the water before it reaches the nozzle. This is a fundamentally different mechanism from a passive hose attachment. The pump does work on the water, adding energy to the system and dramatically increasing pressure. A nozzle attached to a garden hose simply cannot replicate this, because it has no energy source of its own.
What a Nozzle CAN Do
•	Narrow the water stream to increase exit velocity
•	Change the spray pattern from fan to jet
•	Improve the feel and control of water flow
•	Provide a more comfortable grip than a bare hose
What a Nozzle CANNOT Do
•	Multiply the input pressure from a household spigot
•	Generate pressures anywhere near 1,500+ PSI
•	Replace the function of an integrated pump system
•	Remove embedded oil, deep-set mould, or cured stains
The physics limitation here is not a matter of engineering quality or material superiority — it is a matter of fundamental physical law. Bernoulli's principle and the conservation of energy dictate that a passive device cannot create pressure; it can only redistribute existing pressure and velocity. When a nozzle narrows the aperture, velocity increases but total pressure does not. In fact, some pressure is lost due to friction within the nozzle itself. The result is a faster, more focused stream of water — but not a higher-pressure one in the sense that matters for deep cleaning.
For consumers, this physics gap has profound practical implications. Tasks that genuinely require high pressure — such as removing embedded oil stains from a driveway, stripping cured paint from a surface, or eliminating deep-rooted moss from paving — simply cannot be accomplished with a garden hose nozzle, regardless of how well-constructed that nozzle is. The energy required to dislodge such contaminants is far beyond what 40–60 PSI can deliver, even when focused through a narrow aperture. Understanding this limitation is essential before making a purchasing decision.
